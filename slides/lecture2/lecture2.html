Part 1 — Linear Algebra

Linear Algebra

Linear algebra is the math of collections of numbers and the rules that transform them.

Deisenroth et al., MML, Ch. 2.
Systems of Linear Equations
  • A linear equation: variables only to the first power, no products.
  • A system: several such equations in the same unknowns.
  • Goal: find values of the unknowns satisfying all equations simultaneously.

\( 2x + y = 5 \qquad x - y = 1 \)

Geometric View
  • Each linear equation in 2 unknowns is a line.
  • A solution is an intersection point.
  • Three cases:
    • Unique — lines cross
    • No solution — parallel
    • Infinite — coincident

Solving Linear Systems — Gaussian Elimination
  • Write the system as \( A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b} \).
  • Form the augmented matrix \([A \,|\, \mathbf{b}]\).
  • Allowed row operations:
    • swap two rows
    • scale a row by a non-zero constant
    • add a multiple of one row to another
  • Reach row-echelon form, then back-substitute.
Deisenroth et al., MML, Ch. 2.3.

Linear Mappings

A function \(T:V \to W\) is linear if, for all vectors \(x,y\) and scalars \(\alpha,\beta\),

\( T(\alpha x + \beta y) = \alpha\,T(x) + \beta\,T(y) \)

  • ✅ \(T(x) = 3x\) — scaling
  • ✅ \(T(x,y) = (x+y,\ x-y)\) — rotation + scale
  • ❌ \(T(x) = x+1\) — affine, not linear
  • ❌ \(T(x) = x^2\) — nonlinear
Deisenroth et al., MML, Ch. 2.7.

Part 2 — Probability

Reasoning Under Uncertainty

Most interesting problems are noisy: dice rolls, weather, stock prices, a robot's sensor.

Probability is the math that lets us reason carefully when we can't be certain.

We'll come back to this all semester.

  • \(\Omega\) — sample space: all outcomes.

  • \(\mathcal{A}\) — events: subsets we care about.

  • \(P\) — probability measure: \( \mathcal{A} \to [0,1] \):
    • \(P(\Omega) = 1\), \(P(\emptyset) = 0\)
    • countable additivity
Deisenroth et al., MML, Ch. 6.1.
Joint, Marginal, Conditional — Two Coins
  • Joint: \( P(X{=}H, Y{=}T) \)
  • Marginal: \( P(X{=}H) = \sum_y P(X{=}H, Y{=}y) \)
  • Conditional: \( P(Y{=}T \mid X{=}H) = \dfrac{P(X{=}H, Y{=}T)}{P(X{=}H)} \)
🖊️ Board (optional): Build the \(2\times 2\) joint table for two fair coins; compute all three quantities live.
Deisenroth et al., MML, Ch. 6.2.
Continuous Probabilities
  • Continuous outcomes (heights, temperatures) use a PDF \(p(x)\) instead of a PMF.
  • Key twist: \( P(X = a) = 0 \) for any single point.
  • Probability is defined over intervals: \[ P(a \le X \le b) = \int_a^b p(x)\,dx \]

Sum Rule & Product Rule
  • Sum rule (marginalization): \[ P(x) = \sum_y P(x, y) \]
  • Product rule (chain rule): \[ P(x, y) = P(x \mid y)\,P(y) = P(y \mid x)\,P(x) \]

Two workhorses of all probability calculations.

Deisenroth et al., MML, Ch. 6.3.

Bayes' Theorem

\( P(H \mid D) = \dfrac{P(D \mid H)\,P(H)}{P(D)} \)

  • \(P(H)\) — prior
  • \(P(D \mid H)\) — likelihood
  • \(P(H \mid D)\) — posterior
  • \(P(D)\) — normalizing constant

Bayes' theorem is how you update beliefs in light of new evidence.

Expectation
  • The expected value is the long-run average.
  • Discrete: \( \mathbb{E}[X] = \sum_x x\,P(X=x) \)
  • Continuous: \( \mathbb{E}[X] = \displaystyle\int x\,p(x)\,dx \)
  • Fair die: \( \mathbb{E}[X] = \dfrac{1+2+3+4+5+6}{6} = 3.5 \)
  • Linearity (always, even if \(X,Y\) dependent): \[ \mathbb{E}[aX + bY] = a\mathbb{E}[X] + b\mathbb{E}[Y] \]
Deisenroth et al., MML, Ch. 6.4.
Variance

\( \mathrm{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[(X - \mathbb{E}[X])^2] = \mathbb{E}[X^2] - (\mathbb{E}[X])^2 \)

  • How spread out are outcomes around their mean?
  • Units: \(X\) in meters \(\Rightarrow\) \(\mathrm{Var}(X)\) in m².
  • Standard deviation \(\sigma = \sqrt{\mathrm{Var}(X)}\) — back in meters.
  • Fair die: \(\mathrm{Var}(X) \approx 2.92,\ \sigma \approx 1.71\).
Independence
  • Events \(A, B\) are independent if \[ P(A \cap B) = P(A)\,P(B) \]
  • Equivalently, \( P(A \mid B) = P(A) \) — knowing \(B\) doesn't change your belief about \(A\).
  • ✅ Example: two rolls of a fair die.
  • ❌ Non-example: drawing two cards without replacement.
