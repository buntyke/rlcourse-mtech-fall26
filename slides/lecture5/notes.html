Filler F1–F3 — Title, Journey, Acknowledgements

Standard openers. Spend ~2 minutes total.

  • On the journey slide, emphasize that today is a philosophical pivot: until now we estimated values; today we estimate the policy itself. This is the seed idea of policy gradient.
  • Mention that §2.9 (contextual bandits) is short in S&B but disproportionately important — it's the bridge to the rest of the course (MDPs next lecture).

PART A — From Values to Preferences

Slide 1 — Recap

Message: Every method we've seen so far follows the recipe (estimate Q) → (act on Q).

Story:

  • Quick verbal roll call: ε-greedy (Lecture 3), UCB / KL-UCB / Thompson Sampling (Lecture 4). Ask the class to name the one quantity every method maintained. Answer: \(Q_t(a)\).
  • Today we drop \(Q_t(a)\) entirely. Instead we keep a number per arm called the preference \(H_t(a)\), and we let actions be sampled from a distribution defined by these preferences.

Gotcha: Some students will assume \(H\) is "just \(Q\) by another name". It is not — \(H\) has no units and no probabilistic interpretation. It is just a knob.

Slide 2 — Why Preferences?

Message: Preferences are invariant under additive shifts. That's the algebraic property that makes gradient updates clean.

Story:

  • Write on the board: if you replace \(H(a) \leftarrow H(a) + 1000\) for every arm, the softmax outputs the same probabilities. Verify by inspection of the formula.
  • Implication: only differences between preferences matter. We do not need to anchor \(H\) to anything (no "true value").
  • This is what lets us derive a tidy gradient — there's no constraint like "values must equal expected reward" to fight against.

Forward pointer: Mention this is exactly the same property neural-network policy parameters \(\theta\) will have in REINFORCE.

Slide 3 — The Softmax Distribution

Message: The link between preferences and behaviour is the softmax (Gibbs / Boltzmann) distribution.

Story:

  • Start with the formula on the slide. Walk through the three properties:
    1. All probabilities are positive (because exp is always positive).
    2. They sum to 1 (because of the normalisation in the denominator).
    3. Larger \(H \Rightarrow\) larger \(\pi\), but never zero.
  • Point at the right-hand image: as preferences grow, the distribution sharpens to a near-deterministic policy.
  • Temperature comment (optional): if you scale all preferences by \(\beta\) (an inverse-temperature), small \(\beta\) → uniform exploration, large \(\beta\) → near-greedy. S&B don't introduce \(\beta\) but it's worth a sentence for the curious.

Slide 4 — Question #1 (Softmax = Sigmoid for k=2)

Message: The softmax is just the multi-class generalisation of the logistic / sigmoid.

Board derivation: start from

\[ \pi_t(2) = \frac{e^{H_t(2)}}{e^{H_t(1)} + e^{H_t(2)}}. \]

Divide numerator and denominator by \(e^{H_t(2)}\) (or \(e^{H_t(1)}\)):

\[ = \frac{1}{e^{H_t(1) - H_t(2)} + 1} = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-(H_t(2) - H_t(1))}} = \sigma\!\big(H_t(2) - H_t(1)\big). \]

Why this matters: every "softmax classifier" you've seen in deep learning is a generalisation of logistic regression. Same algebraic skeleton. Now the same softmax is going to be our policy.

PART B — The Gradient Bandit Algorithm

Slide 5 — Definition

Message: Three moving parts: preferences, softmax sampling, and a stochastic-gradient update keyed off a baseline.

Story: Read the definition box aloud. Then unpack three words:

  • "Preference" — a knob with no units.
  • "Softmax" — turns the knobs into probabilities.
  • "Stochastic-gradient rule" — the update we'll meet on Slide 6, which we'll later prove (Slides 11–16) is exactly stochastic gradient ascent on \(\mathbb{E}[R_t]\).

Slide 6 — The Update Rule (Eq. 2.12)

Message: Two equations — one for the chosen arm, one for everyone else. Same scalar \((R_t - \bar R_t)\) sets the magnitude.

Story walk-through:

  • Look at the chosen arm's update. The factor \((1 - \pi_t(A_t))\) is positive but small if the chosen arm was already very likely (already-confident arm gets a small kick).
  • Look at the non-chosen arms. They all get pushed in the opposite direction, weighted by their current probability \(\pi_t(a)\). High-probability competitors get pushed down more.
  • Conservation law: the sum of changes across all arms is exactly zero (because \((1 - \pi(A_t)) + \sum_{a \neq A_t}(-\pi(a)) = 0\)). So the centre of mass of \(H\) is unchanged — only the spread changes. Mention this is consistent with the additive-shift invariance from Slide 2.

Common confusion: "Why does an unchosen arm get updated at all?" Because the softmax couples them — increasing \(H(A_t)\) by itself would shift mass off the others, so the gradient wants those others to drop in compensation.

Slide 7 — The Baseline

Message: \(\bar R_t\) is just the running mean of all rewards (not per-arm).

Story:

  • Write the incremental form on the board (it's on the slide too).
  • Why subtract it? Two ways to motivate:
    1. Algebraic: it doesn't change the expected gradient (we'll prove this in Part C).
    2. Variance: it dramatically reduces the noise in each update — the stress test will be Slide 18 (the +4 shift).
  • For nonstationary problems, replace the \(1/t\) step with a constant \(\alpha\) (callback to §2.5).

Slide 8 — Pseudocode

Message: It's about 12 lines of code.

Story: Read it line by line. Stress that the only per-step computation is one softmax, one sample, one categorical observation, and a vectorised update.

Gotcha: in a real implementation, the inner loop "for each arm a" can be vectorised. The branch "if a == A_t" turns into the indicator \(\mathbb{1}_{a = A_t}\) and can be computed with a one-hot vector. Mention this if you have programmers in the room.

Slide 9 — Worked Numeric Example

Message: Make the update tangible with one step.

Board sanity check: before the step, \(\sum_a H_t(a) = 0\). After the step, the changes are \(+0.067, -0.033, -0.033\). Sum: \(+0.067 - 0.033 - 0.033 = 0.001\) (rounding) ≈ 0. The conservation law holds.

Pedagogy: ask the class "what would happen if \(R_t\) were \(-1.0\) instead?" Answer: arm 2's preference would drop, the other two would rise. Update direction follows the sign of \((R_t - \bar R_t)\).

Slide 10 — Question #2 (Baseline necessity)

Discussion before revealing the answer:

  • Ask the class: "if every reward is in \([+9, +11]\), and \(\bar R_t = 0\), what direction does every update push?"
  • (Answer: every selected arm's preference goes up; every unselected arm's preference goes down. This is not learning which arm is best — it's just "whichever arm I pulled, that one is now more likely.")
  • With a baseline near 10, the sign of \((R_t - \bar R_t)\) reflects whether this arm did better than the average arm — that's the actual learning signal.

Punchline: the baseline turns "absolute rewards" into "relative advantages". This is the same idea as the advantage function in actor–critic methods.

PART C — Why It Works: Stochastic Gradient Ascent

This is the longest chunk and the most math-heavy. The slides use a vertical stack so you can pace it. Total time ~25 minutes. Two boardwork moments are flagged: Slides 12 and 14.

Slide 11 — The Goal

Message: State the dream: do gradient ascent on \(\mathbb{E}[R_t]\). State the obstacle: we don't know \(q_*\). State the plan: rewrite the gradient as an expectation we can sample.

Story: The whole derivation is one trick repeated in different guises — the log-derivative / score-function trick. We're going to peel it apart slowly.

Slide 12 — Step 2: Introduce a Baseline

🖊️ BOARDWORK — write this out fully

Slide claim: Adding any baseline \(B_t\) (independent of \(x\)) to the gradient sum doesn't change its value.

Board derivation (write this out cleanly):

Start from the chain rule applied to \(\mathbb{E}[R_t]\):

\[ \frac{\partial \mathbb{E}[R_t]}{\partial H_t(a)} = \frac{\partial}{\partial H_t(a)} \sum_x \pi_t(x)\,q_*(x) = \sum_x q_*(x)\,\frac{\partial \pi_t(x)}{\partial H_t(a)}. \]

Now insert a baseline \(B_t\) that does not depend on \(x\):

\[ \sum_x q_*(x)\,\frac{\partial \pi_t(x)}{\partial H_t(a)} = \sum_x \big(q_*(x) - B_t\big)\,\frac{\partial \pi_t(x)}{\partial H_t(a)} + B_t \underbrace{\sum_x \frac{\partial \pi_t(x)}{\partial H_t(a)}}_{=\,0}. \]

Why is the marked sum zero? Differentiate the identity \(\sum_x \pi_t(x) = 1\) with respect to \(H_t(a)\):

\[ \frac{\partial}{\partial H_t(a)} \sum_x \pi_t(x) = \frac{\partial 1}{\partial H_t(a)} = 0, \]

so the sum of partials is zero.

Conclusion (write below the derivation):

\[ \frac{\partial \mathbb{E}[R_t]}{\partial H_t(a)} = \sum_x \big(q_*(x) - B_t\big)\,\frac{\partial \pi_t(x)}{\partial H_t(a)}, \qquad \text{for any } B_t. \]

Pedagogical note: circle the "= 0" on the board. This single observation is the entire reason gradient bandit and REINFORCE work in practice. The baseline doesn't bias the gradient.

Slide 13 — Step 3: Multiply and Divide by π

Slide claim: Algebraic trick to turn the sum into an expectation.

Board (you can do this verbally if time is short, or write it out):

Multiply each term by \(\pi_t(x) / \pi_t(x) = 1\):

\[ \sum_x \big(q_*(x) - B_t\big)\,\frac{\partial \pi_t(x)}{\partial H_t(a)} = \sum_x \pi_t(x) \cdot \big(q_*(x) - B_t\big) \cdot \frac{1}{\pi_t(x)}\frac{\partial \pi_t(x)}{\partial H_t(a)}. \]

Recognise the \(\sum_x \pi_t(x) [\,\cdot\,]\) as expectation over \(A_t \sim \pi_t\):

\[ = \mathbb{E}_{A_t \sim \pi_t}\!\left[\big(q_*(A_t) - B_t\big)\,\frac{1}{\pi_t(A_t)}\frac{\partial \pi_t(A_t)}{\partial H_t(a)}\right]. \]

Now use the tower property \(\mathbb{E}[R_t \mid A_t] = q_*(A_t)\) and pick the convenient baseline \(B_t = \bar R_t\):

\[ = \mathbb{E}\!\left[(R_t - \bar R_t)\,\frac{1}{\pi_t(A_t)}\frac{\partial \pi_t(A_t)}{\partial H_t(a)}\right]. \]

Why this matters: we have rewritten an expectation over \(q_*\) (which we don't know) as an expectation over actually-observed rewards \(R_t\) (which we do see). That's the whole game.

Slide 14 — Step 4: Softmax Derivative

🖊️ BOARDWORK — write this out fully

Slide claim: \(\dfrac{\partial \pi_t(x)}{\partial H_t(a)} = \pi_t(x)\,(\mathbb{1}_{a=x} - \pi_t(a))\).

Board derivation (write this out — students will see it again in deep learning):

Let \(Z = \sum_y e^{H_t(y)}\), so \(\pi_t(x) = e^{H_t(x)} / Z\). Apply the quotient rule with respect to \(H_t(a)\):

\[ \frac{\partial \pi_t(x)}{\partial H_t(a)} = \frac{\dfrac{\partial e^{H_t(x)}}{\partial H_t(a)}\, Z \;-\; e^{H_t(x)}\,\dfrac{\partial Z}{\partial H_t(a)}}{Z^2}. \]

Compute each derivative:

  • \(\dfrac{\partial e^{H_t(x)}}{\partial H_t(a)} = \mathbb{1}_{a=x}\,e^{H_t(x)}\) (zero unless we are differentiating with respect to the same index).
  • \(\dfrac{\partial Z}{\partial H_t(a)} = \dfrac{\partial}{\partial H_t(a)}\sum_y e^{H_t(y)} = e^{H_t(a)}\) (only the \(y = a\) term survives).

Substitute:

\[ \frac{\partial \pi_t(x)}{\partial H_t(a)} = \frac{\mathbb{1}_{a=x}\,e^{H_t(x)}\,Z - e^{H_t(x)}\,e^{H_t(a)}}{Z^2} = \mathbb{1}_{a=x}\,\frac{e^{H_t(x)}}{Z} - \frac{e^{H_t(x)}}{Z}\cdot\frac{e^{H_t(a)}}{Z}. \]

Recognise the softmaxes:

\[ = \mathbb{1}_{a=x}\,\pi_t(x) - \pi_t(x)\,\pi_t(a) = \pi_t(x)\,\big(\mathbb{1}_{a=x} - \pi_t(a)\big). \qquad \blacksquare \]

Pedagogical anchors:

  • This is the same Jacobian that appears in cross-entropy backprop: \(\partial \text{softmax}_x / \partial \text{logit}_a = \pi_x(\delta_{ax} - \pi_a)\).
  • For \(a = x\) (own derivative): \(\pi_x(1 - \pi_x)\) — same shape as the Bernoulli variance, no coincidence.
  • For \(a \neq x\): \(-\pi_x \pi_a\) — competing classes drag each other down.

Slide 15 — Step 5: Plug In and Recover Eq. 2.12

Slide claim: Substituting the softmax derivative into the expectation produces exactly the gradient bandit update.

Board:

Substitute Slide 14's identity into the expression from Slide 13:

\[ \frac{1}{\pi_t(A_t)}\frac{\partial \pi_t(A_t)}{\partial H_t(a)} = \frac{1}{\pi_t(A_t)} \cdot \pi_t(A_t)\,(\mathbb{1}_{a=A_t} - \pi_t(a)) = \mathbb{1}_{a=A_t} - \pi_t(a). \]

So

\[ \frac{\partial \mathbb{E}[R_t]}{\partial H_t(a)} = \mathbb{E}\!\left[(R_t - \bar R_t)\,(\mathbb{1}_{a=A_t} - \pi_t(a))\right]. \]

A single sample of this expectation, plugged into the gradient ascent rule \(H_{t+1}(a) = H_t(a) + \alpha \cdot (\text{sample})\), gives exactly Eq. 2.12.

Punchline (say it out loud): "What looked like an ad-hoc heuristic — push the chosen arm up, push the others down — is literally one step of stochastic gradient ascent on the expected reward."

Slide 16 — Step 6: Takeaways

Story (4 short beats):

  1. The expected update equals the true gradient \(\Rightarrow\) standard SGA convergence theory applies (Robbins–Monro step-size conditions, etc.).
  2. The baseline \(B_t\) is not unique. Any function independent of the action is admissible. Bad baselines just mean noisy updates.
  3. Optimal baseline (advanced, optional): the variance-minimising choice is not \(\bar R_t\) — it's a weighted average. S&B Bibliographic Note 2.8 references Greensmith et al. for the curious. \(\bar R_t\) is "good enough".
  4. The whole derivation is the policy-gradient theorem in miniature. When we get to REINFORCE in Unit IV, we will redo this proof with one substitution: replace \(R_t\) by the return \(G_t\). Nothing else changes structurally.

Slide 17 — Question #3 (Why baseline doesn't bias)

Drawing it out: ask the class to articulate the answer in their own words. Then write the one-liner on the board:

\[ \sum_x \frac{\partial \pi_t(x)}{\partial H_t(a)} = 0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \mathbb{E}[\text{baseline term}] = 0. \]

That's the entire argument. Variance changes; mean does not.

PART D — Empirical Behaviour

Slide 18 — The +4 Shift

Message: S&B test the algorithm on a deliberately adversarial problem for the no-baseline case.

Story: all reward distributions are shifted up by +4. Now every reward is comfortably positive. With \(\bar R_t = 0\), every update pushes the selected arm up — there is no signal about which arm is better. The baseline rescues us.

Slide 19 — With vs Without (Fig. 2.5 full slide)

Message: Empirically, the no-baseline curve flatlines at a worse level. The with-baseline curve climbs to near-optimal.

Things to point out on the figure:

  • The no-baseline curve is roughly the same shape as the with-baseline curve, just shifted down — it is still learning something, just not the right thing.
  • Both curves start at the same place because at t = 1 the algorithm has no preference yet.

Slide 20 — Parameter Study (Fig. 2.6)

Message: Like every algorithm in Ch. 2, gradient bandit has an inverted-U vs \(\alpha\). It is reasonably robust over an order of magnitude.

Story: Sutton & Barto's overall verdict in Fig. 2.6 is that UCB is the strongest on the (stationary, mean-zero) testbed. But that's setting-specific — gradient bandit is the one that generalises most cleanly to function approximation, which is why it matters more than the empirical comparison suggests.

Slide 21 — Question #4 (\(B_t = 1000\))

Discussion:

  • (a) Yes, expectation is unchanged — that's exactly Slide 17.
  • (b) Variance explodes. Each \((R_t - 1000)\) is a ~−1000-magnitude number, and \(\alpha \cdot (-1000) \cdot (\mathbb{1} - \pi)\) is a giant swing per step. Training curves will be jagged and convergence (in number of samples) will be wildly slow.

Numerical illustration on the board (if time): with \(\alpha = 0.1\), one step changes \(H(A_t)\) by \(\approx 0.1 \cdot (-1000) \cdot (1 - 0.5) = -50\). After this one step, the softmax is essentially deterministic on the other arms — the system is unstable.

PART E — Contextual Bandits / Associative Search

Slide 22 — From Nonassociative to Associative

Message: All bandit problems we've seen so far have a single situation. Real life has many.

Story: Open with a question: "Is it the same headline that's best for every user?" No. The best action depends on the context.

Slide 23 — Coloured Slot Machines

Message: S&B's intuition pump for §2.9.

Story: the slot machine displays a colour, and the colour determines which underlying bandit task you're playing. With no signal, you're trying to track a randomly shifting target. With the signal, you can learn one policy per colour.

Quick exercise (verbal): "If there are 3 colours and 4 actions, how many decisions does the policy specify?" Answer: 3 actions, one per colour — the policy is a table of size 3 (or a function from colour to action).

Slide 24 — Definition

Message: State it formally. Three pieces: context \(C_t\), action \(A_t\), reward \(R_t\). The policy is now \(\pi : C \to A\).

Gotcha: No state transitions. Crucial bridge property — actions affect only the immediate reward, just like a bandit. This is why contextual bandits are not full RL.

Slide 25 — Where They Sit (Comparison Table)

Message: Three rungs on a ladder.

Story: Walk down the table column by column.

  • k-armed bandit: one situation, find the best arm.
  • Contextual bandit: many situations, find the best arm in each.
  • MDP: many situations that you transition between based on your actions.

The jump from "bandit → contextual bandit" adds state. The jump from "contextual bandit → MDP" adds dynamics (state transitions caused by actions).

Slide 26 — Application Lens

Message: Most "RL in production" stories are actually contextual bandits.

Story (concrete examples):

  • News feed ranking (Yahoo!, MSN).
  • Ad auction selection.
  • Recommendation systems.
  • A/B testing with personalisation.

These all share: many contexts, many candidate actions, immediate single-step feedback, no need to plan ahead. Why contextual bandits and not MDPs? Because the per-impression decision is short-horizon, and the engineering burden of an MDP solver is much higher.

Slide 27 — Question #5 (Exercise 2.10)

Numeric walk-through (do this at the board):

Without context: compute the marginal expected value of each arm.

  • \(\mathbb{E}[\text{arm 1}] = 0.5 \cdot 10 + 0.5 \cdot 90 = 50\).
  • \(\mathbb{E}[\text{arm 2}] = 0.5 \cdot 20 + 0.5 \cdot 80 = 50\).
  • Indifferent. Best expected reward = 50 per step.

With context: in each case, pick the better arm.

  • Case A (prob 0.5): pick arm 2 (value 20).
  • Case B (prob 0.5): pick arm 1 (value 90).
  • Best expected reward = \(0.5 \cdot 20 + 0.5 \cdot 90 = 55\) per step.

Information value = 55 − 50 = 5 per step. Knowing the context is worth +5 per pull.

Pedagogical anchor: this is the simplest possible quantification of the value of information — which is itself a deep topic in Bayesian decision theory.

PART F — Wrap-up & Bridge

Slide 28 — Comparison Table

Story: Summarise the four flavours we've now seen. The point is not to memorise the table — it's to internalise that all four are different mechanisms for the same job: balance exploration and exploitation.

Slide 29 — Bridge to Policy Gradient

Message: The whole derivation we just did is REINFORCE in disguise.

Mapping (write on the board):

Gradient banditREINFORCE / actor–critic
preference \(H_t(a)\)parameterised policy \(\pi_\theta(a \mid s)\)
softmax over preferencessoftmax over network outputs
\(R_t\)return \(G_t = \sum_{k} \gamma^k R_{t+k+1}\)

no statefull state \(s\)
no transitionsfull Markov dynamics

Punchline: "Today's lecture is the cleanest, smallest version of policy gradient. The rest of the course is just adding more moving parts to the same skeleton."

Slide 30 — Exit Check

Read aloud. Emphasise the next-lecture bridge: actions starting to affect the next state — that's the qualitative jump to MDPs.

Slide 31 — Question #6 (Exit Question)

Discussion structure:

  • (a) Contextual bandit. Reasoning: per-visitor click feedback is immediate (no temporal credit assignment), the action is one-shot per visitor, but the optimal headline depends on user features.
  • (b) A plain gradient bandit ignores context. Promote the preferences to functions of context: \(H_t(a) = \theta_a^\top \phi(C_t)\). The exact same derivation goes through with a chain rule: \[ \theta_a \leftarrow \theta_a + \alpha\,(R_t - \bar R_t)\,(\mathbb{1}_{a = A_t} - \pi_t(a))\,\phi(C_t). \] This is the springboard to policy gradient with linear function approximation — and from there, to deep policy networks.
